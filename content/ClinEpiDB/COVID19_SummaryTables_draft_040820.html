Hydroxychloroquine

Recommendation

Evidence of Efficacy &/or Effectiveness

Limitations of Evidence

  • Hydroxychloroquine 400 mg PO q12h x 1 day then 400 mg daily for 4 days can be considered in hospitalized and/or pregnant patients with COVID19.

  • Clinical data for hydroxychloroquine are limited. Patients with risk factors for progression to severe disease (immunocompromising conditions and medications, structural lung disease, hypertension, cardiovascular disease, diabetes, and age >60 years old) may also be at increased risk for side effects.

  • Not recommended for outpatient use as the risk outweighs the theoretical benefit, as patients cannot be closely monitored.

  • Consider with caution in patients with prolonged QT at baseline and/or other QT-prolonging medications.

  • A baseline ECG or review of telemetry tracing is recommended to assess QT in patients with age>50, prior or current evidence of cardiac disease, or other agents that can prolong QT if an ECG has not been performed within the past 3 months or if changes have occurred in risk factors for QT prolongation since the last ECG.

  • If benefits of hydroxychloroquine outweigh risks in patients with prolonged QT or on other medications that prolong QT, reassess QTc by ECG or telemetry on or around day #3 of hydroxychloroquine in these high-risk patients. Ongoing monitoring may be needed at the discretion of the primary team.

  • Low-risk patients do not need baseline ECG or ongoing monitoring unless otherwise dictated by clinical care.

  • Mixed evidence of efficacy against SARS-CoV-2 from two small randomized clinical trials in China. Among 62 patients (31 treated with hydroxychloroquine) hydroxychloroquine was associated with decreased duration of fever and cough.1 However, in a separate trial among 30 patients (15 treated with hydroxychloroquine) there was no difference in viral clearance between treatment and control.2

  • Multiple clinical trials are ongoing.

  • Limited clinical data.

  • Two randomized clinical trials with small sample sizes demonstrated mixed evidence of efficacy against SARS-CoV-2.


Chloroquine

Recommendation

Evidence of Efficacy &/or Effectiveness

Limitations of Evidence

  • Not currently recommended as treatment for SARS-CoV-2.

  • A clinical trial among 22 patients with SARS-CoV-2 found that patients treated with chloroquine (n=10) had viral clearance, improvement in chest CT, and faster discharge compared to patients treated with lopinavir/ritonavir (n=12).3

  • A short communication describing a multicenter study in China found that chloroquine was superior to control in SARS-CoV-2.4 However, no methods, data, or analyses have been published.

  • In vitro data suggests that chloroquine can inhibit SARS-CoV-2.5

  • Animal models and in vitro studies against non-COVID19 coronaviruses show inhibitory effects of chloroquine, although these effects were dependent on both the timing of administration and the dose.6-13

  • Only one small clinical trial has been published.

  • Insufficient evidence to determine the efficacy of chloroquine against SARS-CoV-2.

  • Safety and efficacy have not been established in patients with COVID-19.


Hydroxychloroquine vs Chloroquine

Recommendation

Evidence of Efficacy &/or Effectiveness

Limitations of Evidence

  • There is currently insufficient evidence to recommend the use of chloroquine over hydroxychloroquine against SARS-CoV-2.

  • Mixed data from in vitro studies evaluating hydroxychloroquine and chloroquine against SARS-CoV-2. Conflicting findings regarding the comparative potency of the two drugs.14,15 Both drugs decreased viral replication in a dose-dependent manner.15

  • No clinical trial data.

  • Comparative effectiveness studies have not been conducted in patients with SARS-CoV-2.



Azithromycin + Hydroxychloroquine

Recommendation

Evidence of Efficacy &/or Effectiveness

Limitations of Evidence

  • Azithromycin should not be added to hydroxychloroquine only to treat SARS-CoV-2.

  • Single, non-randomized study16,17 with 6/36 patients receiving hydroxychloroquine + azithromycin demonstrated improved virologic clearance among HCQ/azithro group (versus 14 HCQ and 16 control) at day 6. However, the study had many important limitations in design and analysis.

  • No randomized clinical trials.

  • Prospective case report among 11 hospitalized patients with severe COVID-19 found no clinical benefit.18

  • Previous research of macrolides in MERS-CoV demonstrated no benefit in mortality or viral clearance.19


Remdesivir

Recommendation

Evidence of Efficacy &/or Effectiveness

Limitations of Evidence

  • Remdesivir is an investigational drug.

  • Treat is among non-critically ill hospitalized patients is through clinical trials with Remdesivir, if the patient qualifies (dosing and duration depend on clinical trial).

  • Remdesivir can be prescribed as compassionate use in pregnancy (200 mg IV x 1 day then 100 mg IV daily for total 10-14 days); however, safety data of remdesivir in pregnant humans is minimal so decision to use remdesivir should be made in conjunction with Maternal Fetal Medicine and Infectious Diseases, as well as shared decision making with the patient. Compassionate use remdesivir is not an FDA-approved medication and should be obtained only through infectious diseases consultation.

  • If you think a patient would qualify for a remdesivir trial, please directly contact Kathleen Degnan (HUP) or William Short (PPMC). Do not alter patient treatment or testing based on the possibility of trial enrollment. Trials are available only if the patient is an inpatient at HUP or PPMC.

  • In SARS-CoV-2, evidence is from a single case report among one patient20 and an in vitro study.5

  • Several trials are currently underway investigating in vivo use in SARS-CoV-2.

  • Animal models and in vitro studies in SARS-CoV-1 and MERS-CoV indicate that Remdesivir reduces viral replication and improves clinical outcomes.21-23

  • Animal studies in SARS-CoV indicate that timing of administration early in course may maximize potential benefit.23

  • Inadequate evidence in SARS-CoV-2.

  • Inadequate in vivo evidence in MERS & SARS-CoV-1.

  • A clinical trial investigating remdesivir in Ebola was discontinued early after demonstrating a decreased mortality benefit relative to other investigational drugs.24

  • Limited evidence in pregnancy.


Ribavirin

Recommendation

Evidence of Efficacy &/or Effectiveness

Limitations of Evidence

  • In critically-ill patients, may consider adding Ribavirin 2g PO x 1 then 600 mg PO q8h to Lopinavir/ritonavir 400 mg/100mg PO q12h x 10-14 days.

  • Not currently recommended as monotherapy for SARS-CoV-2.

  • Ribavirin is contraindicated in pregnancy, males with pregnant partners, and in patients with hemoglobinopathies.

  • Ribavirin carries a risk of hemolytic anemia, which can be severe.

  • Mixed evidence from retrospective studies in MERS. One retrospective study among 44 patients treated with ribavirin + interferon-a-2a saw a reduction in 14 day mortality, but not 28 day mortality.25 However, two other studies found no difference in mortality or viral clearance.26,27

  • Open trial data among 152 patients with MERS demonstrated benefit of lopinavir/ritonavir in combination with ribavirin over ribavirin alone.28

  • There currently is no in vitro or clinical data available for ribavirin against SARS-CoV-2.

  • Little to no data showing benefit when used as monotherapy or with interferon.

  • One study among 90 patients with SARS-CoV-1 found no benefit with ribavirin compared to antibiotics and steroids.29

  • Mixed evidence from retrospective studies in MERS. Two studies among 5 and 349 patients with MERS demonstrated no difference in mortality or viral clearance.26,27



Lopinavir/Ritonavir

Recommendation

Evidence of Efficacy &/or Effectiveness

Limitations of Evidence

  • In critically ill patients, may consider Lopinavir/ritonavir 400 mg/100mg PO q12h x 10-14 days +/- Ribavirin2g PO x 1 then 600 mg PO q8h

  • Assess for drug-drug interactions before starting. Main side effect is GI intolerance. Monitor LFTs while on therapy.

  • Administer with food for optimal absorption.

  • Liquid formulation is reserved for patients without oral access or ability to swallow tablets. Liquid formulation should be administered only through PVC- or silicone-based feeding tubes.

  • Crushing tablets is not recommended due to 50% reduction in exposure and should only be considered if no other options are available and after consulting with an infectious diseases specialist.

  • A retrospective study among 33 patients with COVID-19 in China found that combined lopinavir/ritonavir + arbidol (n=16) was associated with clinical improvement and decreased viral detectability when compared to lopinavir/ritonavir (n=17) alone.30

  • A retrospective cohort study among patients with SARS-CoV-1 in Hong Kong found improved mortality and decreased intubation rates among patients who received lopinavir/ritonavir + ribavirin (n=75) as initial treatment, compared to control (n=634). No difference was found when lopinavir/ritonavir + ribavirin was used as salvage therapy.31

  • Animal and in vitro studies in SARS-CoV-1 and MERS found improved clinical and virologic response with lopinavir/ritonavir compared to control.28,32

  • In a randomized clinical trial among 199 patients with SARS-CoV-2 in China, there was no difference in mortality, time to clinical improvement, or viral clearance among patients who receive lopinavir/ritonavir (n=99) compared to control (n=100).33

  • A case series among 18 patients with COVID-19 in Singapore found equivocal data on clinical outcomes and viral shedding among 5 patients receiving lopinavir/ritonavir. 4/5 patients discontinued treatment early due to side effects.34

  • Animal and in vitro studies in SARS-CoV-1 and MERS found that lopinavir and ritonavir were not as efficacious when administered individually.35,36

  • In comparison to remdesivir, combined lopinavir, ritonavir and interferon beta had decreased antiviral efficacy against MERS-CoV in vitro and in vivo.22


Corticosteroids

Recommendation

Evidence of Efficacy &/or Effectiveness

Limitations of Evidence

  • Not recommended as treatment for SARS-CoV-2. Corticosteroids may worsen outcomes and prolong duration of viral shedding. However, they could be considered if patient has another compelling indication.

  • Betamethasone 12mg IM Q24H x 2 doses for fetal lung maturity may be considered in cases at very high risk of preterm delivery before 34 weeks. These cases should be reviewed with Maternal Fetal Medicine prior to administration of betamethasone.

  • A retrospective cohort study among COVID-19 patients who developed ARDS (n=84) found that methylprednisolone treatment was associated with a decreased risk of death.37

  • No clinical trial data in SARS-CoV-2.

  • currently no definite evidence of benefit

  • Descriptive studies of corticosteroids in SARS-CoV-2 do not suggest a clear benefit.38,39

  • Studies of corticosteroids in treating other viral illnesses including MERS, SARS, and influenza found no mortality benefit and noted reduced viral clearance in addition to other known corticosteroid side effects.40-42



Tocilizumab

Recommendation

Evidence of Efficacy &/or Effectiveness

Limitations of Evidence

  • Not currently recommended as part of treatment for SARS-CoV2

  • Single, retrospective, single arm, observational study of 21 patients in China showed improvement of fever, oxygenation, and chest imaging. However, this study has many important design limitations and a number of confounding variables.

  • 4 case reports, to date, demonstrating predominantly good outcomes in patients receiving tocilizumab for other indications.43-46

  • No randomized clinical trial data (1 ongoing in China)

Hydroxychloroquine

Hydroxychloroquine

Mechanism: Metabolite of chloroquine. Anti-malarial 4-aminoquinoline 

Experimental Studies

Chen Z, Hu J, Zhang Z, et al. Efficacy of hydroxychloroquine in patients with COVID-19: results of a randomized clinical trial. medRxiv. 2020:2020.2003.2022.20040758.

Question

Is hydroxychloroquine (HCQ) efficacious in treatment of patients with COVID-19?

Findings

  1. Compared to standard treatment, patients receiving HCQ had a significant reduction in duration of fever (3.2 days vs. 2.2 days) and cough

  2. Compared to control group, a larger proportion of patients in the control group had improvement in pneumonia on imaging between days 0 and 6 (54.8% vs. 80.6%)

  3. Two patients in HCQ group had mild adverse events

Setting & Design

Randomized (double-blinded) clinical trial of patients with COVID-19 in Wuhan, China

  • 62 patients received standard treatment: oxygen therapy, antiviral agents, antibacterial agents, and immunoglobulin, +/- corticosteroids

  • Treatment group (N=31): additionally received 5 days of hydroxychloroquine 400 mg daily

  • Control group (N=31): standard treatment, only

Outcome

  • Time to clinical recovery and clinical characteristics 5 days after enrollment

  • Change in Chest CT between day 0 and day 6

Patient Population

62 patients admitted with RT-PCR confirmed COVID-19 and pneumonia on chest CT at Renmin Hospital, Wuhan University

  • Inclusion criterial: Age ≥ 18 years; SaO2/SPO2 ratio > 93% or PaO2/FIO2 ratio > 300

  • Exclusion criteria: Does not meet safe follow-up criteria due to severe and critical illness or safety concerns; retinal diseases; arrhythmias; Severe liver disease (e.g., Child-Pugh score C or AST> twice the upper limit); Pregnant or breastfeeding; Severe renal failure [GFR < 30 mL/min] or receiving renal replacement therapy; possibility of being transferred to another hospital within 72 hours; received any trial treatment for COVID-19 within 30 days

Limitations

A large proportion of screened participants were excluded, no measure of viral load. Choice of endpoints suboptimal & poor reporting

Chen Jun LD, Liu Li, Liu Ping, Xu Qingnian, Xia Lu, Ling Yun, Huang Dan, Song Shuli, Zhang Dandan, Qian Zhiping, Li Tao, Shenn Yinzhong, Lu Hongzhou. A pilot study of hydroxychloroquine in treatment of patients with common coronavirus disease-19 (COVID-19). J Zhejiang Univ (Med Sci). 2020;49(1):0-0.

Question

Is hydroxychloroquine (HCQ) safe & efficacious in the treatment of patients with COVID-19?

Findings

No difference between treatment and control group

  • At day 7, there was no significant difference in viral clearance between treatment and control group (93% vs. 87%; P>0.05), as measured by nasopharyngeal swab

  • No difference median hospital duration, time to become afebrile, or adverse events (diarrhea, LFT abnormality)

  • At two weeks no deaths in any patients.

Radiologic progression in CT in 33.3% HCQ group and 46.7% control group

Setting & Design

Randomized clinical trial among 30 patients with COVID-19 at the Shanghai Public Health Clinical Center in China

  • Treatment group (N=15): 5 days of hydroxychloroquine 400 mg daily

  • Control group (N=15): conventional treatment, only

Outcome

Viral carriage in respiratory phalangeal swab at day 7

Patient Population

30 treatment naïve patents hospitalized at Shanghai Public Health Clinical Center in China

Limitations

Details taken from abstract as paper was not in English
Chloroquine

Chloroquine

Mechanism: Against Coronavirus: Alkalize the phagolysosome, which hampers the low pH-dependent steps of viral replication, including infusion and uncoating. Other mechanisms are poorly explained. Interferes with glycosylation of cellular receptors of SARS-CoV.5,47

Experimental Studies

Gao J, Tian Z, Yang X. Breakthrough: Chloroquine phosphate has shown apparent efficacy in treatment of COVID-19 associated pneumonia in clinical studies. Biosci Trends. 2020;14(1):72-73.

Question

Short communication describing multicenter trial to evaluate the safety and efficacy of chloroquine phosphate in coronavirus pneumonia

Findings

Results have demonstrated that chloroquine phosphate is superior to control treatment in inhibiting the exacerbation of pneumonia, improving lung imaging findings, promoting a virus negative conversion, and shortening disease course

  • No adverse events noted

  • Chloroquine was recommended for inclusion in the next version of the Guidelines for the Prevention, Diagnosis, and Treatment of Pneumonia Caused by COVID-19 issued by the National Health Commission of the People's Republic of China

Setting & Design

Brief communication regarding 20 ongoing clinical trials across 10 hospitals in Wuhan, Jingzhou, Guangzhou, Beijing, Shanghai, Chongqing, and Ningbo, China to evaluate the efficacy and safety of chloroquine and hydroxychloroquine

Outcome

Communication of preliminary findings from ongoing clinical trials in China

Patient Population

100 patients across 10 hospitals

Limitations

Review article: no data or analyses presented

  • Study design, analyses, and validity of findings are not able to be evaluated

Hong W. Combating COVID-19 with Chloroquine. J Mol Cell Biol. 2020.

Question

Does chloroquine have a therapeutic benefit among patients with COVID19?

Findings

Chloroquine was effective among patients with SARS-CoV-2

  • Percentage of patients who transitioned to a negative PCR were slightly higher at day 7, 10, and 14 in chloroquine group

  • On chest CT, more improvement was noted in the chloroquine group.

  • Patients were discharged faster in chloroquine group

    • On day 14, 10/10 (100%) patients in the chloroquine group were discharged, compared to 6/12 (50%) patients in the lopinavir/ritonavir group

Setting & Design

Clinical trial among 22 patients in Singapore with SARS-CoV-2 positive PCR treated with either chloroquine or lopinavir/ritonavir for 10 days and followed-up for a total of 14 days

  • 10 patients (3 severe, 7 moderate) received 10 days chloroquine 500mg twice daily

  • 12 patients (5 severe, 7 moderate) received 10 days of lopinavir/ritonavir 400mg/100mg bid

Outcome

Viral RNA by RT-PCR, lung function by computerized tomography (CT) scanning, and T cell counts

Patient Population

22 patients with SARS-CoV-2

Limitations

Small sample size

Baseline characteristics and statistical analyses were not presented

In Vitro

Wang M, Cao R, Zhang L, et al. Remdesivir and chloroquine effectively inhibit the recently emerged novel coronavirus (2019-nCoV) in vitro. Cell Res. 2020;30(3):269-271.

Question

Does chloroquine have in vitro antiviral efficacy against a clinical isolate of SARS-CoV-2?

Findings

Demonstration of efficacy of chloroquine in vitro against SARS-CoV-2

  • In vitro activity of chloroquine against 2019-nCoV in monkey kidney cells at low- micromolar concentrations (half-maximal effective concentration [EC50], –1.13 mM; EC90 was 6.90 mM) high cytotoxicity (cytotoxic concentration [CC50],>100), and high selectivity (selectivity index [SI], >88.5) suggesting effective concentration likely to be achieved in non-human primates

    • EC90 can be clinically achievable as demonstrated in plasma of RA patients who received 500 mg

  • Time of addition assay demonstrated chloroquine functioned at both entry and at post-entry stages of the 2019-nCoV infection in Vero E6 cells

Setting & Design

In vitro study evaluating Vero E6 cells (ATCC-1586) infected with 2019-nCoV (clinical isolate nCoV-2019BetaCoV/Wuhan/WIV04/2019) at a MOI of 0.05 and then treated with different doses of antiviral

Outcome

In vitro antiviral efficacy:

  • Mean inhibition of virus yield (quantified by qRT-PCR) and cytotoxicity (CCK-8 assay)

Determination of when Remdesivir acts, relative to viral entry, using a time-of-addition assay

Patient Population

In vitro cell lines:

  • African green monkey kidney Vero E6 cell line

  • Human liver cancer Huh7 cell line

Limitations

Difficult to extrapolate in vivo activity and dosing from in vitro experiments

Evidence in Other Coronaviruses 

Barnard DL, Day CW, Bailey K, et al. Evaluation of immunomodulators, interferons and known in vitro SARS-coV inhibitors for inhibition of SARS-coV replication in BALB/c mice. Antivir Chem Chemother. 2006;17(5):275-284.

Question

Does chloroquine reduce the replication of SARS-CoV in vivo?

Findings

In vitro: Chloroquine inhibited virus replication (IC90, 11)

  • Found to be more toxic than has been previously reported

In vivo: Chloroquine did not significantly reduce the replication of SARS-CoV virus in the lungs of infected mice

Setting & Design

In vitro study evaluating Vero 76 cells infected with SARS-CoV, strain Urbani (200300592), and treated with different doses of antiviral

In vivo study mice were pretreated with chloroquine and then exposed to SARS-CoV, strain Urbani (200300592)

  • Toxicity-controlled with five mice treated at each dose of compound or placebo

  • Lungs were assayed for viral titer

Outcome

90% reduction in virus yield (IC90)

Patient Population

African green monkey kidney cells (Vero 76)

Specific pathogen-free BALB/c female mice

Limitations

Difficult to extrapolate in vivo activity and dosing from in vitro experiments

Animal models are an imperfect analog to humans

Burkard C, Verheije MH, Wicht O, et al. Coronavirus Cell Entry Occurs through the Endo-/Lysosomal Pathway in a Proteolysis-Dependent Manner. PLOS Pathogens. 2014;10(11):e1004502.

Question

How do coronaviruses enter the host cell membrane?

Findings

Inhibition of endosome maturation with chloroquine severely diminished luciferase expression when the inhibitors were added prior to infection

  • Much smaller effects were observed when chloroquine was added at 2hpi

    • The inhibitors (chloroquine) mainly affect MHV entry.

Setting & Design

In vitro use of recombinant mouse hepatitis coronavirus (MHV) expressing reporter genes and a novel replication-independent fusion assay to investigate fusion and entry.

Perturbation of endosome maturation by the addition of inhibitory agents (chloroquine) corroborated the importance of trafficking of MHV virions through the endocytic pathway.

Outcome

Determine cell entry pathway

Patient Population

HeLa-mCC1a cells

Limitations

Difficult to extrapolate in vivo activity and dosing from in vitro experiments

de Wilde AH, Jochmans D, Posthuma CC, et al. Screening of an FDA-approved compound library identifies four small-molecule inhibitors of Middle East respiratory syndrome coronavirus replication in cell culture. Antimicrob Agents Chemother. 2014;58(8):4875-4884.

Question

Does chloroquine exhibit anti-MERS-CoV activity?

Findings

Chloroquine inhibited MERS replication in a dose-dependent manner in the low-micromolar range (EC50 3mM), inhibited SARS replication with EC50 4.1mM, and inhibited human coronavirus 229E with EC50 3.3mM

  • Chloroquine blocked MERS entry at an early step.

Setting & Design

In vitro study of cells infected with MERS-CoV (strain EMC/2012), SARS-CoV, and HCoV-229E, followed by screening of 348 FDA-approved drugs.

Outcome

50% effective concentration (EC50) and compound-specific toxicity (50% cytotoxic concentration [CC50])

Patient Population

Vero, Vero E6, and Huh7 cells


Limitations

Difficult to extrapolate in vivo activity and dosing from in vitro experiments

Keyaerts E, Vijgen L, Maes P, Neyts J, Van Ranst M. In vitro inhibition of severe acute respiratory syndrome coronavirus by chloroquine. Biochem Biophys Res Commun. 2004;323(1):264-268.

Question

Does chloroquine inhibit of the replication SARS-CoV in vitro ?

Findings

Chloroquine inhibited SARS-CoV in Vero E6 cells

  • IC50 of chloroquine for antiviral activity (8.8 ± 1.2 lM) was significantly lower than its cytostatic activity; CC50 (261.3 ± 14.5 lM), with a selectivity index of 30

  • Chloroquine administration could be delayed for up to 5 hours pos-inoculation, without a significant drop in antiviral activity

Setting & Design

In vitro study evaluating Vero E6 cells infected with SARS-CoV-2 and then treated with different doses of antiviral

Outcome

Cytostatic activity of chloroquine

Patient Population

Vero E6 cells

Limitations

Difficult to extrapolate in vivo activity and dosing from in vitro experiments

Animal models are an imperfect analog to humans

Question

Can chloroquine prevent in vitro replication of human corona virus and death in mice?

Findings

In vitro: Chloroquine inhibited HCoV-OC43 replication in HRT-18 cells, with an EC50 of 0.306 mM

In vivo: Chloroquine, acquired transplacentally or via maternal milk, prevented HCoV-OC43-induced death in newborn mice

  • Survival rates declined in a dose-dependent manner: Highest survival rate (98.6%) when mother mice were treated daily with a concentration of 15 mg/kg chloroquine, 88% survival when treated with 5 mg/kg, 13% survival when treated with 1 mg/kg

Setting & Design

In vitro study evaluating HRT-18 cells infected with HCoV-OC43 and treated with different doses of antiviral

In vivo study of female pregnant mice injected subcutaneously different dilutions of chloroquine beginning 2 days before labor, with subsequent inoculation of five-day old mice

Outcome

Viral replication

Death of mice pups

Patient Population

HRT-18 cells

Five-day old pups born from six-week-old male and female C57BL/6 mice

Limitations

Difficult to extrapolate in vivo activity and dosing from in vitro experiments

Animal models are an imperfect analog to humans

Inhibited the release of HCoV- 229E into supernatants significantly in a dose-dependent manner

Using human fetal lung cell lines infected with HCoV-229E and treated with varying doses of antiviral.

Human fetal lung cell lines

Question

Does chloroquine inhibited FIPV replication in vitro and in cats with experimentally induced FIP?

Findings

In vitro: Chloroquine had an inhibitory effect against the replication of FIP virus in vitro

  • Demonstrated inhibitory and anti-inflammatory effect of chloroquine versus FIP

In vivo: Cats receiving chloroquine had better clinical course than untreated cats

  • Alanine aminotransferase levels increased in the chloroquine-treated groups.

In vitro: feline monocytes cultured chloroquine for two days

  • Virus titers and TCID50 were calculated

  • Late group: saline subcutaneously administered prior to FIPV inoculation, with chloroquine (10 mg/kg) after inoculation

  • Control: saline subcutaneously administered prior to and after FIPV inoculation

    • One cat in the control group died prior to FIPV inoculation and was excluded

Outcome

Clinical signs, and we measured their body temperature and weight

Patient Population

Felis catus whole fetus-4 (fcwf-4) cells

Nine SPF cats

Hydroxychloroquine vs Chloroquine

Hydroxychloroquine vs Chloroquine

In Vitro

Liu J, Cao R, Xu M, et al. Hydroxychloroquine, a less toxic derivative of chloroquine, is effective in inhibiting SARS-CoV-2 infection in vitro. Cell Discov. 2020;6:16.

Question

Is hydroxychloroquine as efficacious as chloroquine in treating SARS-CoV-2 infection?

Findings

Hydroxychloroquine can efficiently inhibit SARS-CoV-2 infection in vitro

  • There was no significant difference in the cytotoxic concentration (CC50) of hydroxychloroquine and chloroquine (273.3 vs. 249.5)

The anti-SARS-CoV-2 activity of hydroxychloroquine may be less potent compared to chloroquine

  • At all MOIs (0.01, 0.02, 0.2, and 0.8), the EC50 for chloroquine (2.71, 3.81, 7.14, and 7.36 microM) was lower than that of hydroxychloroquine (4.51, 4.06, 17.31, and 12.96 microM)

  • The differences in EC50 values were statistically signiﬁcant at a MOI of 0.01 (P<0.05) and MOI of 0.2 (P<0.001)

  • The selectivity index of hydroxychloroquine was lower than chloroquine at all MOIs

Setting & Design

Hydroxychloroquine and chloroquine were evaluated against SARS-CoV-2 in vitro in African green monkey kidney VeroE6 cells

  • Cytotoxicity measured by CCK8

  • Dose–response curves of the two compounds against SARS-CoV-2 were determined at four different multiplicities of infection (MOIs) by quantiﬁcation of viral RNA copy numbers in the cell supernatant at 48h post infection (p.i.).

Outcome

Antiviral effect of hydroxychloroquine and chloroquine against SARS-CoV-2 infection in vitro

Patient Population

African green monkey kidney VeroE6 cells

Limitations

In vivo activity in animal model is an imperfect analogue to humans

Yao X, Ye F, Zhang M, et al. In Vitro Antiviral Activity and Projection of Optimized Dosing Design of Hydroxychloroquine for the Treatment of Severe Acute Respiratory Syndrome Coronavirus 2 (SARS-CoV-2). Clin Infect Dis. 2020.

Question

What are antiviral and prophylactic profiles of hydroxychloroquine and chloroquine in vitro?

Findings

In vitro, Hydroxychloroquine (EC50=0.72 μM) was more potent than chloroquine (EC50=5.47 μM)

  • At 24 & 48 hours, the EC50 values for hydroxychloroquine (24hours, 6.25; 48 hours, 5.85) were lower than for chloroquine (24hours, >100; 48 hours, 18.01)

PBPK models conducted in lung fluid found that hydroxychloroquine was more potent than chloroquine

  • Model demonstrated that 5 days oral hydroxychloroquine sulfate (loading dose of 400 mg twice daily on day 1, followed by 200 mg given twice daily for 4 days) may be sufficient for SARS-CoV-2 infection

Tocilizumab

Mechanism51-54: Tocilizumab is a recombinant, humanized, anti-human IL-6 receptor monoclonal antibody that binds and inhibits soluble and membrane bound IL-6 receptors, thereby inhibiting signal transduction. Reduction in IL-6 levels is believed to curb inflammatory response and associated sequelae of inflammation.

  • The pathogenesis of SARS, MERS, and COVID-19 have all been associated with occurrence of cytokine storm and elevation of interleukins, tumor necrosis factor, etc. & IL-6 has been identified as being elevated with all 3 coronavirus strains.

  • Severe COVID-19 disease is associated with a cytokine profile resembling haemophagocytic lymphohistiocytosis (sHLH; increased IL-2, IL-7, G-CSF, IFN-gamma inducible protein 10, monocyte chemoattractant protein 1, macrophage inflammatory protein 1-alpha, and TNF-alpha)
